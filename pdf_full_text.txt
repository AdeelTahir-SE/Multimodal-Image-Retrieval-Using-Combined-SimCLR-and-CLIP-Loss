Abstract — Financial fraud in FinTech systems is one of the key reason behind billions of dollars lost annually in 
financial services. Existing solutions for fraud detection follow two paradigms; Static ensemble models which achieve 
high accuracy on known fraud pa tterns but fail to identify novel fraud patterns. On the other hand, dynamic adaptive 
systems which are flexible in their behaviour but suffer from interpretability and generalization. Neither approach can 
satisfy the requirements of accurate, adaptable, interpretable, and private detection at once. In this paper, we present a 
three layered hybrid architecture built on a combination of both approaches. The first layer uses a cost -sensitive 
XGBoost model with SHAP interpretability, achieving an ROC -AUC of 0.9952 on ULB Credit Card Fraud and 
outperforming SMOTE methods on all primary metrics. .The second layer employs a reinforcement learning Proximal 
Policy Optimization (PPO) agent for detecting structurally ambiguous/novel fraud cases trained on Gaus sian noise, 
reaching 100% of recall on structurally ambiguous frauds that were not detected by Layer 1. Layer 3 allows for privacy-
preserving federated learning through multiple simulated banks with weighted FedAvg and differential privacy noise 
injection (ε=1.0. Furthermore, Layer 1 validation is done using the IEEE -CIS Fraud Detection benchmark dataset, 
showcasing the domain-independent capabilities of the system. The proposed approach, in our knowledge, is believed 
to be the first framework which uses st atic ensemble classification, reinforcement learning with an adaptive policy, 
SHAP-based explainability, and differential privacy at the same time in a single deployable architecture.  
 
Index Terms : Fraud Detection, XGBoost, Reinforcement Learning, Proximal Policy Optimization, Federated 
Learning, Differential Privacy, SHAP, FinTech, Class Imbalance, Credit Card Fraud.  
I. INTRODUCTION 
    Financial services are quickly moving to digital channels, 
which has caused a significant rise in fraud. Just credit card 
fraud alone results in tens of billions of dollars in losses 
worldwide each year, and the rise of online payments creates 
an ever -expanding attack surface for malicious actors. 
Fraudsters continually alter their strategies to evade financial 
institutions' fraud -detection mechanisms, thus creating an 
arms race between detection and deception. 
    Current fraud detection systems show a fundamental 
divide in their design and architecture. Static ensemble 
approaches, which mainly include gradient -boosted tree 
models such as XGBoost, achieve very high accuracy on 
known fraud distributions, but thes e models are 
architecturally frozen post -training. After being deployed, 
they cannot adapt to novel fraud patterns that arise after their 
training cutoff. On the other hand, dynamic adaptive systems, 
which are based on reinforcement learning or behavioral 
modeling, can detect novel patterns through continuous 
interaction with the environment but pose challenges for 
interpretability (via SHAP explanations), regulatory 
compliance, and cross-institutional knowledge sharing. 
    Another systemic challenge is the isolated nature of 
financial fraud data, specifically transactions made by actual 
users. Privacy regulations such as GDPR and PCI -DSS don't 
allow institutions to share raw transaction records, even 
though collaborative intelligence could greatly improve 
detection rates for all institutions deploying fraud detection 
and classification mechanisms. A novel fraud attack vector 
may be encountered b y one institution months before others 
do; however, the regulatory framework m akes knowledge 
transfer between financial institutions extremely difficult. 
  
    In this paper, we propose a three -layer hybrid fraud 
detection framework that tackles these problems 
simultaneously. Our approach integrates:  
(1) a cost-sensitive XGBoost classifier alongside mandatory 
SHAP explainability as a high -throughput static detection 
layer; (2) a PPO -based reinforcement learning agent with 
Gaussian-augmented training as an adaptive layer for 
ambiguous and novel fraud pa tterns; and (3) a differentially 
private federated learning module that enables model weight 
sharing among institutions without exposing any raw data 
related to transactions.  
    The system is evaluated on the ULB Credit Card Fraud 
dataset with cross-domain validation on the IEEE-CIS Fraud 
Detection dataset. 
 
The key contributions of our work are: 
 
• A dual -approach hybrid architecture that routes 
transactions using static and adaptive layers based on 
confidence thresholds, thus combining the precision of

II. LITERATURE REVIEW 
A. Static Ensemble Learning 
Al Ahmed et al. [1] in his work, suggested a pipeline that 
combines K -Means clustering for customer segmentation, 
Gradient Boosting for loan prediction, and Random Forest for 
fraud detection in one FinTech application. Although 
theoretically connected to each other, models are trained and 
tested separately without propagating information between 
models. Furthermore, all models are trained on static 
historical snapshots and have no means of adapting to 
changing patterns of fraud over time - a key aspect of o ur 
framework. 
Majumder [2] used XGBoost for classification on 
Kaggle's Credit Card Fraud dataset using a preprocessing 
pipeline including SMOTE class balancing, IQR outlier 
removal, Min -Max normalization, and PCA dimensionality 
reduction.The model achieved 99.32% accura cy, 99.31% 
precision, and 99.42% recall. This solution is based on a 
rather outdated (by a decade) historical data sample, does not 
have a validation on any live deployment and an explanation 
framework, and is using synthetic data generated with 
SMOTE algo rithm that may not reflect real fraud 
distributions. Our work directly improves on this baseline by 
replacing SMOTE with cost -sensitive learning and adding 
mandatory SHAP explanations, achieving ROC -AUC of 
0.9952 with superior precision. 
B. Dynamic Adaptive Systems 
Adebayo [3] uses Proximal Policy Optimization as a fraud 
detection agent that constantly receives transaction state 
information from its environment, executes actions like 
accept/flag/block, and learns to refine its policy using reward 
information — all without any further need for training.The 
proposed method works better than any static classifier when 
dealing with frauds unknown during training. However, the 
centralized single -agent architecture is incompatible with 
real-world decentralized financial dat a, and the agent can 
experience long cold start periods after deployment. 
In recent research [4], the IEEE group combines temporal 
sequences through LSTM models and relationships between 
entities by utilizing Graph Neural Networks in order to detect 
behavioral anomalies in high -frequency transactions., 
achieving 110ms detection latency.  The crucial weakness of 
the system is the long time required to update the model after 
discovering a new type of attack i.e. 1.9 seconds, during 
which time tens of th ousands of transactions occur without 
any screening. Also, a large behavioral history is required for 
the GNN creating a severe cold start problem for a new user. 
C. Privacy-Preserving Federated Detection 
The Federated Learning technique [5] is used to train 
models on non -centralized datasets while avoiding data 
sharing. Within the context of fraud detection, organizations 
use federated learning by sharing gradients/weights of 
models and not actual transactions. The method of combining 
gradients through the FedAvg algorithm [6], invented by 
McMahan et al., has become a conventional one to merge 
federated models. Combining federated learning with 
differential privacy [7] offers formal privacy guarantees, due 
to which such a system can comply with GDPR and PCI-DSS 
regulations. We believe that currently, there is no existing 
research paper that covers all three paradigms - static 
ensemble, adaptive RL, and differentially private federation, 
in one unified system. 
III. DATASETS AND DATA PREPROCESSING 
A. Datasets 
Two publicly available datasets are used in this study. The 
ULB Credit Card Fraud Detection dataset is the primary 
dataset that we use to show the hybrid framework working, 
while the IEEE -CIS Fraud Detection dataset is the second 
dataset for validating and finding limitations in our approach. 
Table I provides a comparative summary. 
The ULB dataset contains transactions made by European 
cardholders in September 2013. Features V1 to V28 are the 
result of PCA transformation that has already been applied by 
the original institution in order to anonymize the sensitive 
customer data. Only the Amount and Time columns are in 
their original form, which can be interpreted. There is an 
extreme class imbalance (492 fraud out of 284,807 
transactions) in this dataset (0.172%), which makes it a 
standard benchmark for imbalanced learning research, which 
is evident of it being used in a lot of other research works. 
The IEEE -CIS dataset was released as part of a Kaggle 
competition sponsored by Vesta Corporation. Unlike ULB, 
all the features in this dataset are interpretable, including 
transaction amount, product category, card type, device 
information, and address dat a, supplemented by identity 
verification features from a separate file. It also has a higher 
fraud rate (3.5%) and a richer feature space, which makes it a 
much more complex classification problem. 
 
B. Data Preprocessing 
For the ULB dataset, we followed the pipeline proposed 
by Majumder [2] with two deliberate improvements. The 
Amount and Time features are scaled using StandardScaler 
before model training. IQR-based outlier removal is applied

dimensionality that is manageable by PPO's default 
MlpPolicy network. 
 
 
 
D. Synthetic Data Generation 
To counter the underrepresentation of structurally 
ambiguous fraud cases in the ULB dataset (8 cases with Layer 
1 predicted probability below 0.80), we applied Gaussian 
noise augmentation exclusively for Layer 2 training. For each 
of the 8 hard cases, 20 synthetic cases are generated by adding 
zero-mean Gaussian noise with standard deviation 0.1 to the  
original feature values. Thus, 160 synthetic training samples 
were produced for layer 2 training alongside the existing 
fraud cases from Layer 1 and 5000 legit cases selected from 
the entire dataset. These synthetic cases are used only for 
training, while evaluation is performed exclusively on the 
original 8 unmodified hard cases that are held out from 
training process. 
 
IV. PROBLEM FORMULATION AND CHALLENGES 
A. Class Imbalance 
ULB dataset shows a ratio of genuine versus fraudulent 
transactions at 577:1. A naive algorithm would predict 
everything as legitimate transaction, yielding an accuracy rate 
of 99.83%. Hence, using normal classification accuracy 
metric in this case is mean ingless. The loss function for the 
classifier with respect to class label y ∈ {0,1} and model's 
prediction p̂  is defined by: 
 
 
L = (1/N) Σ [w₁ · (1-yᵢ) · log(1-p̂ ᵢ) + w₂ · yᵢ · log(p̂ ᵢ)] 
 
 
where w₁ and w₂ are the weights assigned to each class 
label respectively. By assigning weight of positive examples 
to 577 times that of negative examples by means of setting

B. The Frozen Knowledge Problem 
Static models trained on historical distributions are not 
able to deal with the issue of distributional changes in real -
life settings. Suppose Fₜ represents the distribution of fraud 
cases at time t. A machine learning model that was trained 
using F₀ will most likely break down if t >> 0 and fraudsters 
have altered their strategy. The non -stationary nature of the 
process is the main reason why Layer 2 uses reinforcement 
learning which updates its policy through reward signals 
rather than fixed labeled datasets. 
C. Data Redaction and Privacy Constraints 
The data on financial transactions are highly regulated in 
terms of privacy. The PCA features in the ULB dataset have 
been anonymized to show how banks operate when they 
collaborate for research purposes while keeping strict privacy 
policies in mind. More importantly, GDPR article 5 and PCI  
 
 
 
DSS requirement 3 stipulate that any un-anonymized data on 
transactions cannot leave the premises of the organization and 
must stay within its internal network, hence making 
collaboration across multiple organizations for research 
purposes impossible. In f act, it is the basis of the federated 
learning method used at the Layer 3 level where 
organizations will be able to exchange weight information 
rather than data, subject to the guarantees of differential 
privacy.  
D. Cold-Start Vulnerability 
Both RL systems and the use of graph theory require some 
record of past behavior to generate accurate predictions. New 
organizations do not have any transaction history; hence, 
there is a very risky period for them when they first appear.  
At the federated layer, a pre -trained global model can 
provide weight information for new organizations. New 
institutions begin with knowledge aggregated across all 
participating banks rather than starting from zero. 
 
 
V. METHODOLOGY: THE HYBRID FRAMEWORK 
 
A. Overall Hybrid Architecture 
Our proposed solution processes each incoming 
transaction through a confidence-based routing pipeline. The 
overall flow is as follows: each transaction is first scored by 
Layer 1 (XGBoost). If the predicted fraud probability falls 
below a lower threshold α  (set to 0.01), the transaction is 
approved directly. If it exceeds the upper threshold β (set to 
0.80), it is blocked directly. Transactions in the uncertainty 
region [α, β] are routed to Layer 2 for adaptive assessment. 
This design makes sure that Layer 1 handles the high -

The routing function can be formally defined as: 
  
decision(x) = { Approve if p₁(x) < α;  
Block if p₁(x) > β;  
π₂(x) if α ≤ p₁(x) ≤ β }  
 
where p₁(x) is Layer 1's fraud probability for transaction x, 
and π₂(x) is Layer 2's policy action. The federated layer 
(Layer 3) operates asynchronously, while periodically 
updating Layer 2's policy weights using cross -institutional 
aggregation. 
 
B. Layer 1: Cost-Sensitive XGBoost with SHAP 
Explainability 
Layer 1 uses XGBoost [8] trained with cost -sensitive 
weighting as the primary detection mechanism. We use 
XGBoost to minimize the regularized objective: 
 
  
L(Φ) = Σᵢ l(yᵢ, ŷᵢ) + Σₖ Ω(fₖ) 
  
    where l is the logistic loss function, Ω(f) = γT + (1/2)λ||w||² 
is the regularization term, T is the number of leaves, and w 
are leaf weights. The scale_pos_weight parameter is set to 
577 (the class imbalance ratio), increasing influence of fraud 
samples while the tree is being built. 
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) [9] values are 
computed for every flagged transaction to provide regulatory-
grade explainability. For a prediction f(x),  
the SHAP explanation assigns each feature j a value φⱼ 
satisfying: 
  
f(x) = φ₀ + Σⱼ φⱼ 
  
    where φ₀ = E[f(x)] is the base value and φⱼ represents the 
marginal contribution of feature j. This decomposition allows 
human-interpretable explanations for individual fraud flags 
which is a mandatory requirement under GDPR Article 22 for 
automated decision-making. 
C. Layer 2: Adaptive PPO Reinforcement Learning 
Layer 2 considers fraud detection as a Markov Decision 
Process (MDP) that can be defined by the tuple (S, A, P, R, 
γ), where S is the state space of transaction feature vectors, A 
= {Approve, Flag, Block} is the discrete action space, P is the 
transition probability, R is the reward function, and γ is the 
discount factor.

asymmetric cost of fraud detection errors:

cost of implementing differential privacy is a 10.42% recall

VII. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE SCOPE 
    We identified following limitations in our approach and 
these limitations represent directions for future research: 
    Privacy vs. Performance Tradeoff Adding differential 
privacy noise (ε=1.0) to federated weights cost us 10.42% in 
recall. That's a real price to pay for privacy guarantees. One 
direction worth exploring is making the noise budget smarter 
by applying he avier noise to weight components that carry 
less signal, and lighter noise where it matters most. A flat 
epsilon across all weights is a blunt instrument. 
    Layer 2 Doesn't Scale Well to High-Dimensional Data The 
PPO agent worked well on ULB's 30 features but fell apart 
on IEEE -CIS's 218. The default MlpPolicy network is too 
small to learn any meaningful patterns across that many 
dimensions in a reasonable  training window. Applying PCA 
before the RL environment helped partially, but future 
implementations should look at larger policy networks or 
transformer-based architectures that handle high-dimensional 
tabular data more naturally. 
    Simulated Federated Learning We split one dataset 60/40 
to simulate two banks. This is a common approach in 
federated learning research but doesn't capture what real 
multi-institutional deployment looks like. Two banks in 
different countries with diffe rent customer demographics, 
transaction volumes, and fraud profiles would tell a much 
more interesting story. Testing on heterogeneous institutional 
data is the natural next step. 
    Routing Thresholds The 0.01 and 0.80 confidence cutoffs 
were chosen by looking at the probability distribution. These 
were not learned from data. A proper implementation would 
optimize these thresholds on a validation set, or make them 
adaptive based o n current fraud rates and system load. A 
threshold that works well on a quiet Tuesday might be wrong 
during a fraud spike. 
    Bias in Fraud Labels Fraud labels in real datasets show 
which transactions were actually investigated and 
investigation decisions aren't always neutral. If certain 
customer segments were historically over -investigated, the 
model learns to flag those se gments more aggressively 
regardless of actual fraud signal. Any production deployment 
of this system should include a fairness audit before going 
live. 
    Real-World Deployment Gap: We conducted all 
evaluations in an offline batch settings. Real -world 
deployment will latency constraints, concept drift, and 
adversarial adaptation by fraudsters. Future work should test 
this approach in an online streaming environment with 
concept drift detection (e.g., ADWIN or Page-Hinkley tests), 
triggering targeted retraining.  
VIII. CONCLUSION 
    We presented  a three -layer hybrid fraud detection 
framework that addresses the fundamental limitations of both 
static ensemble and dynamic adaptive detection paradigms. 
By combining cost -sensitive XGBoost with SHAP 
explainability, PPO reinforcement learning with Gaussian -
augmented training, and differentially private federated 
learning, our approach achieves what no single paradigm can 
deliver in isolation: high accuracy on known patterns, 
adaptability to novel fraud, regulatory -grade explainability, 
and privacy -preserving cross -institutional knowledge 
sharing. 
    The ROC -AUC score for the ULB Credit Card Fraud 
dataset for Layer 1 is 0.9952, surpassing SMOTE based 
models by 13 percentage points in precision scores. Layer 2 
identifies all the cases of ambiguous frauds missed by Layer 
1. The entire system is able to detect almost 99% of frauds in 
total. The federated learning algorithm exhibits consistent 
convergence after three iterations, the cost of 10.42% recall 
achieved at the cost of epsilon=1.0 formally quantifying the 
utility-privacy tradeoff. 
This architecture  is, as far as we know, the first to bring 
together all four capabilities in a practical manner. The 
modular structure of the architecture facilitates the updating 
of individual layers independently, thereby allowing 
institutions to implement components progressively based on 
their regulatory setting and technological capabilities.
